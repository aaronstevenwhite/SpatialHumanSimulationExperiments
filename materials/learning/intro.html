In this experiment, you'll learn a new word by reading sentences that contain that word. This word is not one you already know. After you've read these sentences, you'll make some judgments about how similar the new word is to words you do already know. For instance, in the first portion of the experiment, you might get the sentence below.

  • The secretary blorped the letter.

After seeing this sentence, you might have some idea what blorp means, but it's probably unclear exactly what: maybe it means something like write, send, read, mail, shred, etc. The next sentence might exclude some of these possibilities.

  • My mom blorped me a cake recipe.

After reading the second sentence, you might be pretty sure that blorp doesn't mean anything like shred and you might think it's less likely that it means something like write (unless you know the speaker's mom writes her own recipes). But blorp could still mean something like send or mail.

Some of the sentences you get may have some nonsense words in them. For instance, instead of the second sentence above, you might get the following sentence.

  • My blicket blorped me the flixop.

But even though you don't know what either blicket or flixop mean, you might still be pretty sure that blorp means something like send or mail.

The sentences will change on their own after 6 seconds, so make sure to pay attention the entire time; otherwise, you might miss some. After some of the sentences in the first portion of the experiment, you will be given a memory task. This task will involve choosing from a list the word that actually occurred in the previous sentence. For instance, after seeing The secretary blorped the letter, you might be given the following list.

  1. box
  2. book
  3. letter
  4. clerk
  5. table

In this case, you would choose word 3, since only that word occurs in the sentence. You can indicate your choice either by pressing the number key associated with it or by clicking on the word.

In the second portion of the experiment, you will be given pairs of words along with a 7-point similarity scale. Your job will be to rate the similarity between the meanings of the two words from very dissimilar (1) to very similar (7).

For instance, if you received the words blorp and send, you might respond with a high similarity rating—perhaps a 6 or a 7—since you were pretty sure blorp meant something like send. On the other hand, if you received the words blorp and shred, you might respond with a low similarity rating—perhaps a 1 or a 2—since the second sentence made you think that blorp didn't mean anything like shred.

Similarly, if you received the two words you already know, like eat and devour, you might respond with a high similarity rating—perhaps a 6 or a 7—since the meanings of eat and devour are quite similar. And if you received the words kill and kiss, you might respond with a low similarity rating—perhaps a 1 or a 2—since the meanings of kill and kiss are quite dissimilar.

The experiment will begin with a short practice to get you used to the task. After this, a message will tell you that you are moving on to the experiment.
